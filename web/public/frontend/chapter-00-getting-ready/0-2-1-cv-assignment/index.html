    0.2.1 Assignment: Research and build your CV¶

    This is your first real assignment. You are not just reading here. You will produce an actual document that you keep, update, and one day send to an employer or a client. That makes this one of the most practical things in the whole course.

    By the end of this assignment you will have:

    • A clear understanding of what a CV is and why it matters right now
    • Experience researching something on your own, the way developers actually work
    • A real one-page CV saved as a PDF, ready to share

    Step 1: Understand what a CV is and why it matters¶

    A CV (Roman Urdu: taaruf naama, yani woh document jo tumhara hunar aur kaam ek jagah pe batata hai) is often the first thing a client or employer sees before they meet you. It is a one-page summary of who you are professionally: your skills, your projects, your education, and your contact details.

    Think of it this way. If you apply for a freelance project online, the client will likely receive ten or twenty applications. They will spend about thirty seconds on each one. Your CV is those thirty seconds. A clear, honest, well-laid-out page gets you to the next round. A messy, vague, or dishonest one does not.

    Even before you finish this course, having a CV matters:

    • You can apply for internships or part-time work as you learn.
    • You can show it to family and community contacts who might know of work.
    • Building it now means you only have to update it later, not start from scratch when you are busy with real projects.

    Step 2: Research like a developer¶

    Developers solve problems by searching first. No one knows everything. The skill is finding good answers fast. Here is how to research CV formats the same way.

    Search terms to try¶

    Open Google and try these exact phrases, one at a time:

    • CV format for students Pakistan
    • fresh graduate CV sample 2024
    • software developer CV no experience
    • front-end developer resume beginner

    Look at several results for each search. Do not stop at the first one. You are trying to spot patterns: what do the good ones have in common?

    Places to find real templates¶

    Where to look What you will find How to get there
    Microsoft Create Free, professional Word templates create.microsoft.com/en-us/templates/resumes
    Canva Visual, easy-to-customise layouts canva.com/resumes/templates
    Google Docs template gallery Clean, ready-to-use Docs templates Open Google Docs, click "Template gallery" at the top
    LinkedIn sample CVs Real profiles from real people Search front-end developer Pakistan on LinkedIn and read profiles

    Judging good vs bad examples¶

    When you look at a CV, ask yourself:

    Question Good sign Bad sign
    Can I read it in thirty seconds? Clear headings, short bullet points Walls of text, tiny font
    Does it fit on one page? Yes, nothing wasted Two or three pages with filler
    Are the contact details visible? Name and email at the top Buried in the middle
    Does it list real, specific skills? "HTML, CSS, JavaScript" "Good communicator, hard worker"
    Are there project links? GitHub links, live demo links "Projects: various"
    Is the formatting consistent? Same font, same spacing throughout Mixed fonts, random bolding

    Spend about ten minutes on this step. Save or bookmark one or two examples that you think are well done. You will use them as reference.

    Step 3: Know the sections of a strong beginner CV¶

    A beginner CV does not need to be long. It needs to be clear and honest. Here are the sections that matter, and what to put in each:

    Section What to include What to leave out
    Name and role Your full name, and the role you want: "Front-End Developer" A photo is optional in Pakistan; a headshot is common here but not required
    Contact Phone, email, city (Sahiwal, Lahore, etc.), and links to GitHub and LinkedIn if you have them Your full home address is not needed
    Summary Two or three sentences: who you are, what you are learning, and what you want Do not write "I am a hardworking person who loves challenges". Write what you can actually do.
    Skills Specific tools and languages you actually know: HTML, CSS, Word, Excel, Google Docs, basic internet search Do not list "Microsoft Office" vaguely. List what you have used.
    Projects Even one project counts. Write the name, one line describing it, and a link if it is online. For now you can write "in progress" or "coming soon". Do not list things you have not built at all.
    Education Your highest qualification, the institution name, and the year. Include this Bano Qabil course: "Front-End Development, Bano Qabil Sahiwal, 2024 (in progress)". You do not need to list every grade unless they are strong.
    Experience Any work, even informal. Helping a family business, doing data entry, teaching someone. Be honest about what you did. Do not call yourself a "manager" if you were a helper.

    Step 4: A note on honesty (sidq)¶

    Keep your CV honest

    Never write a skill you do not have. Never inflate a project into something bigger than it was. Do not say you know React if you have only heard of it.

    This is not just about being good. It is about being smart. Every claim on your CV can be tested: an interviewer can ask you to write code live, or give you a task on your first day. A CV that claims too much fails you at the worst moment, when you most need to impress.

    The honest CV of a beginner who is clearly learning fast is more attractive to a good employer than a dishonest CV that falls apart under one question.

    Write what you have. You will have more to write every month.

    Step 5: Build it in Word or Google Docs¶

    Now you actually make it. Follow these steps one at a time.

    Set up the document¶

    1. Open Google Docs or office.com and create a new blank document.
    2. Set the page margins to narrow: in Google Docs go to File > Page setup, set all margins to 1.5 cm or 0.6 inches. In Word go to Layout > Margins > Narrow. This gives you more room on one page.
    3. Choose one clean font for the whole document. Good choices: Calibri, Arial, Georgia, or Roboto (available in Google Docs). Set the base size to 10 or 11 points.

    Add your sections¶

    Work through the sections from the table in Step 3, in this order:

    1. Your name at the top, large (16 or 18 points) and bold.
    2. The role you want, just below your name, slightly smaller (12 or 13 points).
    3. One line of contact info: phone | email | city | github.com/yourname (if you have one yet).
    4. A short horizontal line (--- in Docs, or Insert > Horizontal rule) to separate the header.
    5. Your two-line summary.
    6. Skills, as a short comma-separated list or a two-column list.
    7. Projects section, even if it just says "coming soon" for now.
    8. Education.
    9. Experience, if any.

    Keep it to one page¶

    One page is the rule for beginners. If it spills onto a second page:

    • Reduce the font size by half a point.
    • Tighten line spacing: in Google Docs use Format > Line and paragraph spacing > Single.
    • Cut any sentence that repeats something already said.
    • Remove anything you put in to fill space rather than to inform.

    Make the formatting consistent¶

    Go through the whole document and check:

    • Every section heading is the same size and weight (for example, all bold and 12 points).
    • Every bullet point starts the same way (a verb, or a noun, but not mixed).
    • There is the same amount of space above and below each section.
    • Nothing is underlined unless it is a link. Bold is enough for headings.

    Export to PDF¶

    When you are satisfied:

    1. In Google Docs: File > Download > PDF Document (.pdf).
    2. In Word: File > Save as > PDF, or File > Export > Create PDF/XPS.
    3. Name the file sensibly: firstname-lastname-cv.pdf, for example ali-hassan-cv.pdf. Lowercase, no spaces, use hyphens. This matters because a recruiter's computer may not display file names with Urdu characters correctly.
    4. Open the PDF and check it. Does it look exactly as you intended? Are all the links clickable?

    Done when¶

    Go through this checklist before you move on. Tick each item only when it is genuinely true.

    • I looked at at least three CV templates or real examples before I started building
    • My CV fits on exactly one page
    • My name, role, phone, email, and city are at the top
    • Every skill I listed is something I can actually describe or demonstrate
    • The projects section exists, even if it only says "coming soon"
    • My education includes the Bano Qabil course
    • The font and spacing are consistent throughout
    • I exported it as a PDF with the filename firstname-lastname-cv.pdf
    • I opened the PDF and confirmed it looks correct

    What is next¶

    You have a CV. You will come back and update it many times as you build projects through this course. The next assignment uses Excel or Google Sheets to build something practical: a small system for tracking students, attendance, and results, the kind of thing a real academy actually uses.
