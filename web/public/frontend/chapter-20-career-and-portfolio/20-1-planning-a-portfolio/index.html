    20.1 Planning a portfolio that gets you hired¶

    You finished the course and built real projects. Now you need a place to show them. A portfolio is your home on the web. It tells people who you are and what you can build. This lesson helps you plan it before you write a single line.

    What you'll know by the end¶

    • What a strong junior frontend portfolio looks like in 2026.
    • The 5 sections every good portfolio site needs.
    • How to write about yourself in a plain, warm voice.
    • How to pick the right 3 projects to feature.
    • Why a short writeup matters as much as a live link.
    • How to plan before you build, so you save time later.

    What a strong portfolio looks like in 2026¶

    A good junior portfolio is small and clean. It loads fast. It shows a few real projects with live links. Each project has a short writeup next to it.

    You do not need ten projects. You need three good ones. Quality wins over a long list. A hiring manager spends about a minute on your site. Make that minute count.

    Your site should also be easy to read on a phone. Most people will open it on mobile first. If it looks broken on a small screen, you lose them.

    Why does a portfolio matter so much? Because a CV is a list of claims. A portfolio is proof. A hiring manager can click your live link and see the result in ten seconds. That proof is worth more than any bullet point.

    The 5 sections of a portfolio site¶

    Keep the structure simple. Five clear sections do the job:

    1. Hero. Your name and one line on what you do. For example, "I build clean, fast web apps."
    2. About. A short, human paragraph about you. Three or four sentences is enough.
    3. Projects. Your best 3 projects. Each one has a live link and a short writeup.
    4. Contact. An easy way to reach you. An email and a couple of links work fine.
    5. Footer. Quick links to your GitHub, LinkedIn, and email.

    That is the whole site. Do not add more until you have a reason.

    What hiring managers look for in 30 seconds¶

    Most hiring managers do not read. They scan. They spend the first 30 seconds deciding whether to keep looking or click away. Knowing what they scan for lets you put the right things in the right places.

    What they look for Where they look Why it matters
    Who you are and what you build Hero section First line they read; must be instant and clear
    Live work, not just code Project live links Seeing the real thing beats reading code
    A real person, not a robot About paragraph Warm, honest writing builds quick trust
    Stack they recognise Tech tags on each project Shows you speak the same tools they use
    Mobile friendliness of the portfolio itself Their phone Broken layout = poor first impression
    How you think, not just what you shipped Project writeups Proves you can solve problems, not just follow tutorials
    Easy way to contact you Contact section Removes friction; if it is hard to reach you, they move on

    Keep this table in mind when you plan each section. Every choice you make should serve one of those seven things.

    How to write about yourself¶

    Many students freeze on the About section. They copy a stiff CV. Do not do that.

    Write in plain, warm words. Use first person. Say "I build" and "I enjoy." Skip the buzzwords. Words like "passionate synergy expert" mean nothing.

    Tell people what you build and who you help. For example, "I build web apps for small shops in Pakistan." That is clear and human. It sounds like a real person, not a robot reading a list.

    Here is a quick test. Read your About section out loud. If you would never say those words to someone at a chai stall, rewrite it.

    Choosing 3 projects to feature¶

    Pick finished projects that are live on the web. A deployed project beats a half-done demo every time.

    Choose three that show range. A good mix could be:

    • Your ecommerce capstone, to show a full app.
    • The donation app, to show forms and data.
    • One personal idea, to show your own taste.

    Depth beats a long list. One project you can explain well is worth more than five you cannot. Cut anything you are not proud of.

    Tip

    Feature 3 finished, deployed projects. Do not show 10 half-built demos. Depth wins. A small, strong set of work tells a clearer story than a big messy pile.

    The role of process writeups¶

    A live link shows what you built. A writeup shows how you think. Add a short story next to each project.

    Keep each writeup to a few short lines. Answer four questions:

    • What problem did this project solve?
    • What choices did you make, and why?
    • What part was hard?
    • What did you learn from it?

    This is the part hiring managers care about most. Anyone can copy a tutorial. They want to see how you solve problems. Your writeup proves you can think on your own.

    Did you know

    Many hiring managers read your project writeups before they open the code. A clear story about your choices can earn an interview on its own.

    Planning your sections: a checklist¶

    Use this table before you write a single line of code. Fill each row on paper or in a note file. If you cannot fill a row, you are not ready to build that section yet.

    Section What to plan Done?
    Hero Your full name; your one-line description of what you build [ ]
    About 3 to 4 warm, honest sentences; what you build, who you help, what you want next [ ]
    Project 1 Title; live URL; tech stack; problem it solved; what was hard [ ]
    Project 2 Title; live URL; tech stack; problem it solved; what was hard [ ]
    Project 3 Title; live URL; tech stack; problem it solved; what was hard [ ]
    Contact Email address; GitHub link; LinkedIn link [ ]
    Footer Same links as Contact; the year [ ]

    Try this¶

    Open a notebook or a blank file and plan your portfolio on paper before you code. List the 5 sections, then write your one-line hero text and a four-sentence About paragraph in plain, warm words. Pick the 3 projects you will feature and, for each one, jot down quick answers to the four writeup questions: what problem it solved, what choices you made, what was hard, and what you learned. Use the planning checklist table above to make sure you have everything before you open your code editor.

    Knowledge check¶

    Don't write anything down. Just see if you can answer these in your head. If you can't, scroll back up. That's what this section is for.

    1. What are the 5 sections of a portfolio site?
    2. Why is it better to show 3 projects instead of 10?
    3. What four questions should a project writeup answer?
    4. Why do hiring managers care about writeups, not just live links?

    What's next¶

    You now have a clear plan for your portfolio. You know the sections, the voice, and the projects. Next you will turn that plan into a real, live site.
