# 20.4 First clients and freelancing in Pakistan

You have learned to build real websites. Now you can earn from this skill. Your first client is closer than you think, often right in your own town. This last lesson shows you how to find that client and how to do the work with honesty.

## What you'll know by the end

- How to find your first local clients near you
- How to start with international clients on Upwork, Fiverr, and LinkedIn
- How to price your work in PKR and in USD
- How to agree scope, send an invoice, and take a deposit
- How to keep your client work on the halal path
- How to receive money from abroad and how to keep learning

---

## Start with local clients

Your first clients are often nearby. Look around your own town and your own contacts.

Many small groups need simple websites. Think about these:

- Your local mosque
- A charity like Alkhidmat
- A small shop or restaurant near your home
- A school or a coaching academy
- A family business run by relatives

These groups rarely need a fancy app. They need a clean page that shows who they are, what they do, and how to reach them. You already know how to build that.

Local clients trust word of mouth (Roman Urdu: zubaan se zubaan tak). One happy shop owner will tell another. So your first job leads to your second job, and the chain grows.

Your first project may pay little, or you may build it free for a good cause. That is fine when you start. You gain a real reference and a real piece for your portfolio. Both are worth more than a small fee at this stage.

!!! tip
    Your first local project, even a free one for a mosque or a charity, gives you a real reference and a portfolio piece. People can see your work, and you can show it to the next client. That proof opens the next door.

## Then reach international clients

Once you have one or two projects to show, you can look abroad. Clients overseas often pay in dollars, which goes far in Pakistan.

Three common places to start:

- **Upwork.** You make a profile, then send proposals to posted jobs.
- **Fiverr.** You list a service, called a gig (Roman Urdu: apni service ka aik package), and buyers come to you.
- **LinkedIn.** You can send a short, polite message to a business owner.

Start small. Take simple jobs first, like a one page site or a small fix. Do them well and on time. Then ask the client for an honest review.

Reviews are your reputation (Roman Urdu: naik naam) online. A new freelancer with five good reviews wins more work than one with none. So protect your reviews by doing careful work.

Keep your LinkedIn messages short and respectful. Tell the person who you are, what you noticed about their business, and how you can help. Never spam. A polite message that adds value beats ten copy and paste ones.

## Freelance platforms compared

Choosing where to find work matters. Each platform works differently and suits different situations. This table gives you a clear comparison so you can pick the right starting point.

| Platform | Getting started | Competition | Payment | Best for |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Upwork | Create a profile, write proposals for job posts; first 10 proposals are free each month | High globally, but mid-level for simple web work | Hourly or fixed, via Upwork; Payoneer or direct deposit | Developers who can write a strong proposal and want recurring clients |
| Fiverr | Create gigs describing your service; buyers search and come to you | Very high for basic gigs; less for specific niches | Fixed per order, via Fiverr; Payoneer withdrawal | Beginners offering a clear, packaged service like "landing page in 5 days" |
| LinkedIn | Polish your profile, then message business owners directly; no bidding | Low for direct outreach; most freelancers do not do this | You invoice the client directly; Wise or bank transfer | Developers comfortable with a short outreach message to a real business |
| Local clients | Word of mouth, WhatsApp, walking into a shop | Very low; most have no developer contact at all | Cash or local bank transfer in rupees | Your very first project; builds confidence and a real reference fast |

The table shows a pattern. The platforms with less competition, LinkedIn and local clients, require you to reach out yourself. The ones where clients come to you, Fiverr, have more noise. Both are valid. Start where you feel you can actually take action today.

## Pricing in PKR and in USD

Pricing confuses most new freelancers. Keep it simple at the start.

Local clients pay in rupees. They often expect lower prices, because that is the local market. A small shop site might be a few thousand rupees, or free for a cause.

International clients pay in dollars. The same work can earn much more, because their market is different. This is normal and fair.

When you start, charge by the project, not by the hour. You are still slow, so hourly pricing punishes you. A fixed project price is clear for both sides. The client knows the cost, and you know the goal.

As your portfolio grows, raise your rates. Each finished project makes you faster and more trusted. Higher rates follow real proof, not wishful thinking.

## The application funnel

Getting your first paid project feels slow at the start. That is normal. Think of it as a funnel (Roman Urdu: qaif). You reach many people, a few reply, fewer still get on a call with you, and one of them becomes a client. The numbers look rough at first and improve as your portfolio grows.

## Contracts and invoicing basics

You do not need a lawyer for your first jobs. You need clear writing.

Before you start, agree on three things with the client:

- **Scope.** What exactly will you build, and what is not included.
- **Price.** The full amount and the currency.
- **Timeline.** When you will deliver the work.

Put these in writing, even a simple WhatsApp message. Writing protects both of you. It stops the slow creep where a small site quietly grows into ten pages for the same price.

Send a clear invoice (Roman Urdu: hisaab ki parchi). An invoice is a short document that lists the work, the price, and how to pay. It looks professional and keeps your records clean.

Take a deposit (Roman Urdu: peshgi) before you begin. A common split is half before and half on delivery. The deposit shows the client is serious. It also covers your time if plans change.

!!! warning
    Always agree scope, price, and timeline in writing before you start, even a WhatsApp message counts. A spoken promise is easy to forget. A written line is easy to point to when memories differ.

## A first client, from Sahiwal

Let me tell you about my first paying client. His name does not matter, but the lesson does.

When I was starting out in Sahiwal, a cloth shop owner near my home needed a simple site. He had heard from a cousin that I "did computer work." He was not sure what a website even was. He just wanted people to find his shop and call him.

I was nervous. I quoted him a small price, took half as a deposit, and wrote the plan in a short message. Three pages. His products, his address, his phone number. Nothing more.

I built it in a week. He was so happy that he showed it to two other shopkeepers in the bazaar. Both of them called me within a month.

Here is what that small job taught me. The site was simple, but the trust was not small. I had done honest work, charged a fair price, and finished on time. That reputation, built one careful job at a time, carried me further than any clever code ever did.

## Staying on the halal path

Your skill can earn good money. Make sure that money stays clean.

Turn down work from haram industries. That includes:

- Gambling and betting sites
- Interest based lending, called riba
- Alcohol and other haram products

Refuse deceptive work too. Some clients ask for tricks that fool people. Say no to:

- Fake countdown timers that pressure buyers with a false rush
- Hidden charges that appear only at the last step
- Misleading claims about a product

Keep your own dealings honest as well. Promise only the scope you agreed. Send invoices that match the real work. Do not pad the bill.

Halal income (Roman Urdu: jaiz aur saaf kamai) is not only about the industry. It is about how you treat people in every step. Honest scope, honest prices, honest invoices. That path is slower sometimes, but it lasts.

## Getting paid from abroad

International clients pay in dollars, so you need a way to receive that money in Pakistan. A few common rails work well here:

- **Payoneer.** Popular with Upwork and Fiverr freelancers. You get paid into a Payoneer account, then withdraw to your local bank.
- **Wise.** Good for direct clients who want to send money to you. Low fees and clear rates.
- **Bank wire.** A direct transfer from the client's bank to yours. Slower and pricier, but reliable for larger jobs.

The flow is usually the same. The client pays the platform or sends money to your service. The money lands in your Payoneer or Wise balance. You then move it to your Pakistani bank account in rupees.

Set this up early, before your first international payment. You do not want to scramble when the money is ready to arrive.

## Keep learning after this course

This course gives you a strong base. The real growth comes after, when you keep building and keep reading.

A few resources to return to often:

- **MDN Web Docs.** The reference for HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Trust it.
- **javascript.info.** Clear, deep lessons on JavaScript.
- **Official docs.** When you use a tool or a library, read its own documentation first.

Beyond reading, do two things. Keep building projects, because building teaches what reading cannot. And read other people's code on GitHub, because you learn new patterns by seeing how others solve problems.

You will never know everything. No developer does. The skill is knowing how to find the answer and how to keep going.
