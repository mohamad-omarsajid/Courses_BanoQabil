# Chapter 1: Orientation and setup

<p class="byline" markdown>Muhammad Umar Sajid · Trainer at Bano Qabil Sahiwal · Front End Development and Graphic Design</p>

This is where the real work starts. First you see the kind of websites you will
be able to build. Then you set up your computer like a working developer, learn
to drive the terminal, and meet the browser tools you will use every single day.

Take the install lesson slowly. Once your tools are ready, the rest of the course
flows.

## Lessons in this chapter

- [ ] [1.1 What is frontend development?](1-1-what-is-frontend.md): frontend, backend, and the kinds of sites you can build.
- [ ] [1.2 Install everything](1-2-install-everything.md): WSL2, Ubuntu, VS Code, Node, and Git, with a Windows-native fallback.
- [ ] [1.3 The terminal](1-3-the-terminal.md): move around your computer by typing, and read errors calmly.
- [ ] [1.4 Browser DevTools](1-4-devtools.md): inspect and edit any live web page in the browser.

!!! tip "No rush"
    Setting up tools can take an afternoon, especially on slow internet. That is
    normal. Finish what you can, take a break, and come back to it.

## Mega assignment

!!! bq-assignment "Choose your client and audit a real Pakistani website"
    For the rest of this course you will build a real one-page website for a
    real local business. Pick one now: a karyana store, a salon, a tutor, a
    tailor, a caf&eacute;, your uncle's shop, anyone you actually know who has no
    website or a weak one. By the end of this book, they will have something real.

    **What you build and do**

    - A short **client brief** (half a page): the business name, what they sell, who their customers are, and three things their website must do (for example: show the menu, show location and hours, let people message on WhatsApp).
    - A **DevTools audit** of one live Pakistani site (Daraz, Foodpanda, or your client's competitor). In your journal, note three HTML elements you inspected, one message the Console shows, and one thing that is clearly frontend versus backend.
    - A **verified toolchain**: prove your setup actually works.

    **Done when**

    - [ ] Your client brief is written and names a real business.
    - [ ] `node -v`, `npm -v`, and `git --version` each print a version in your terminal.
    - [ ] VS Code opens a folder and you can type in its integrated terminal.
    - [ ] Your DevTools audit notes are in your learning journal.

    **Stretch goal:** Message the owner and ask what they wish their customers could do online. Real requirements always beat imagined ones.
