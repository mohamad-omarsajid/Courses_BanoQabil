# 0.4 Learning to learn

Coding is not about being clever. It is about good habits and not giving up. The
students who finish this course are rarely the smartest. They are the ones who
show up, practise a little each day, and know what to do when they get stuck.
This lesson hands you those habits.

## What you'll know by the end

- Why doing beats watching, every time
- The right order to learn anything: watch, then read, then do
- A calm routine for when you get stuck
- How to focus in short blocks, and how to beat the urge to put things off

---

## Learn by doing, not just watching

Watching a tutorial and nodding along feels productive. It is not. Most of it is
gone by the next day. This is passive learning.

Active learning is doing the thing yourself. You type the code, you break it, you
fix it. It is slower and it feels harder, and that is exactly why it sticks.

So make this a rule: after every lesson, build the small example yourself,
without copying. Get it wrong, look it up, try again. That struggle is the
learning.

> **Tip: the one-minute test**
>
> After a lesson, close the page and rebuild the example from memory. You will
> forget bits. Good. Looking them up again is how they move into long-term
> memory.

---

## The right order: watch, then read, then do

When you meet something new, learn it in three steps, in this order. Most
beginners get stuck because they only ever do the first step.

1. **Watch one short video** to get the shape of the idea. One video, not ten. A video is great for a first overview, and a poor choice as your only teacher.
2. **Then read** the docs, an article, or a book chapter on the same topic. Reading is slower, and it gives you the depth and the details a video skips. If you want to go deep in this field, you have to become a reader.
3. **Then practise it yourself.** This is the real step. This is where the learning actually happens. The video and the reading were only preparation for this.

Watching ten videos and building nothing leaves you with nothing. One video, one
article, and one thing you built yourself beats it every time.

---

## The 15-minute rule for getting unstuck

Everyone gets stuck. Being stuck is not failing. It is the normal sound of
learning. Here is a routine that keeps you calm.

1. **Read the error.** It often names the exact line and problem.
2. **Check the small things.** A missing bracket or a wrong file name breaks a lot.
3. **Try for 15 minutes.** Wrestle with it honestly. This is where you grow.
4. **Then ask, and ask well.** Say what you expected, what happened, and show your code.

That last point matters. "It doesn't work" is hard to help. "I expected the
button to turn green, but it stayed grey, here is my code" gets you an answer in
seconds.

> **Did you know**
>
> Stepping away often solves the problem. Your brain keeps working in the
> background. Many bugs get fixed in the shower, not at the desk.

## Focus in short, honest blocks

Two small habits carry a lot of weight: short focused blocks, and a daily rhythm.

The most popular focus method in the world is the **Pomodoro** technique. It is
simple: work with full focus for 25 minutes, then take a 5-minute break. After
four rounds, take a longer break. The short timer makes a big task feel small,
and the breaks keep your mind fresh.

A few mind tricks make starting easier, because starting is the hard part:

- **The two-minute rule.** Tell yourself you will work for just two minutes. Once you start, you usually keep going. Starting is the whole battle.
- **Put the phone in another room.** Not face down on the desk. In another room. You cannot resist a notification you never see.
- **One task at a time.** Close the other tabs. Doing one thing well beats doing five things badly.
- **Little and often wins.** Twenty focused minutes a day beats five hours once a week.

Keep a short notes file as you go. Three lines in your own words after each
lesson is enough. Writing it down is a form of active recall, and active recall
is how memory forms.

## Words to code by

Two lines worth remembering, from people who shaped this field:

> "Programs must be written for people to read, and only incidentally for machines to execute."
>
> Harold Abelson, *Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs*

> "Practice isn't the thing you do once you're good. It's the thing you do that makes you good."
>
> Malcolm Gladwell, *Outliers*

Write code that a human can read. And remember that practice is not what you do
after you are good. Practice is what makes you good.

## A word on laziness

There is a short, powerful lesson in our tradition about putting things off.

The companion Ka'b ibn Malik missed the long march to Tabuk. Not because he
doubted, and not because he could not go. He had two healthy camels and every
means to join. He simply kept delaying. "I will get ready tomorrow," he told
himself, day after day, until the army marched without him. He said later that he
kept putting it off, again and again, until it was too late, and a long and
bitter hardship followed. All of it grew from a habit that feels small: **later**.

!!! note "Don't wait for 'later'"
    Laziness (Roman Urdu: susti) rarely looks like a big choice. It looks like one
    small "later" after another. The cure is the two-minute rule: do the lesson
    now, even a little. Effort today is worth far more than a perfect plan for
    tomorrow.

Retold in short from the long hadith of Ka'b ibn Malik, [Sahih al-Bukhari 4418](https://sunnah.com/bukhari:4418).
