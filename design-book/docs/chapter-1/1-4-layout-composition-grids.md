# 1.4 Layout, Composition & Grids

!!! info "Why this lesson?"
    By the end of this lesson you can arrange things on a page so they feel
    balanced and clean. You can also use a simple grid to keep your work tidy.

    *(Roman Urdu: Is sabaq ke baad tum page par cheezein aise laga sako ge ke
    woh saaf aur balance lagein. Aur tum ek aasaan grid se apna kaam tidy rakh
    sako ge.)*

## What you will learn

- What layout and composition mean
- The rule of thirds
- White space, also called negative space
- Alignment, lining things up
- Using a simple grid

## Layout and composition

**Layout** (Roman Urdu: page par cheezein kahan rakhi jayen) is where you place
things on a page. **Composition** (Roman Urdu: saari cheezon ko mila kar ek
khoobsoorat tarteeb banana) is how all those pieces work together as a whole.

Good layout feels calm and clear. The viewer's eye flows from one part to the
next without confusion. You learned hierarchy in lesson 1.1. Layout is where
you put hierarchy into a real arrangement.

## The rule of thirds

Picture your page split into nine equal boxes, with two lines down and two
lines across, like a noughts and crosses board. This is the **rule of thirds**
(Roman Urdu: page ko nau hisson mein baant kar zaroori cheez ko lines ke milne
wali jagah par rakhna).

Put your most important thing near where the lines cross, not dead center.
Off center spots feel more natural and interesting to the eye.

!!! example "Rule of thirds in a photo poster"
    Imagine a poster with a person's photo and a title. Place the person's face
    near a left crossing point. Place the title near a right crossing point.
    The page feels balanced, not stiff.

## White space

**White space** (Roman Urdu: page par khali jagah jahan kuch nahi hota), also
called negative space, is the empty area around and between things. It does not
have to be white. It is just space with nothing in it.

White space is not wasted space. It does real work:

- It gives important things room to stand out.
- It groups related items and separates unrelated ones.
- It makes a page feel calm instead of crowded.

!!! warning "Do not fill every corner"
    New designers fear empty space and fill it up. A crowded page tires the
    eye. Leave room. Empty space makes the rest look better.

## Alignment

**Alignment** (Roman Urdu: cheezon ko ek seedhi line mein lagana) means lining
things up along a shared edge or line. When edges line up, the page looks neat
and planned. When they do not, it looks sloppy.

A simple rule helps a lot.

1. Pick a line, for example the left edge of your text block.
2. Line up your other items to that same line.
3. Avoid placing things at random spots.

Even one strong alignment line makes a messy page look much cleaner.

## Using a simple grid

A **grid** (Roman Urdu: barabar khanon ka ek dhaancha jis par cheezein lagti
hain) is a set of invisible lines that split your page into columns and rows.
You place things along these lines so everything lines up.

You do not need a fancy grid to start. Even a few columns help.

1. Split your page into two, three, or four columns in your mind or your tool.
2. Place text and images inside those columns, not across random spots.
3. Keep equal gaps between columns.

A grid gives you ready made alignment lines. Most clean designs you admire sit
on a grid, even if you cannot see it.

!!! tip "tl;dr"
    Layout is where things go, and composition is how they work as a whole. The
    rule of thirds puts key items near line crossings, not the center. White
    space gives the page room to breathe. Alignment lines edges up for a neat
    look. A simple grid gives you those lines for free.

2. What does the rule of thirds tell you to do?
3. What does negative space do for a design?
4. What does alignment mean?
5. How does a grid help your layout?

## Optional assignment

Find a messy flyer, either online or a photo of one from your area. Redesign it
to be cleaner and easier to read.

Use what you learned: set a clear hierarchy, add white space, line items up on
a shared edge, and place things on a simple grid. Save the before and after so
you can see how much calmer the new one feels.
