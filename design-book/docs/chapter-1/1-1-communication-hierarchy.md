# 1.1 Design = Communication & Hierarchy

!!! info "Why this lesson?"
    By the end of this lesson you can explain what design really does. You can
    also guide a person's eye to the most important thing first.

    *(Roman Urdu: Is sabaq ke baad tum samjha sako ge ke design asal mein kya
    karti hai. Aur tum dekhne wale ki aankh ko pehle zaroori cheez tak le ja
    sako ge.)*

## What you will learn

- Why design is communication, not decoration
- What hierarchy means
- The four tools that build hierarchy: size, weight, color, and space
- How to read the hierarchy in any design you see

## Design is a message

Every design says something. A poster says "come to this event." An app button
says "tap me." A price tag says "this costs 500 rupees." Design is how that
message reaches a person's eyes.

So design is **communication** (Roman Urdu: paigham pohanchana, ek baat samjhana
bina lambi tafseel ke). It is not just making things pretty. Pretty is nice,
but clear comes first.

A good question to ask is simple. What do I want the viewer to know or do? Then
you build the design around that one answer.

!!! tip "Pretty is not the goal"
    A design can look beautiful and still fail. If the viewer cannot find the
    main point fast, the design did not do its job. Clear beats pretty.

## What is hierarchy?

When you look at a design, your eye does not see everything at once. It sees
one thing first, then the next, then the rest. The order in which your eye
moves is the **hierarchy** (Roman Urdu: woh tarteeb jo aankh ko pehle zaroori
cheez dikhati hai).

Good designers control this order on purpose. They make the most important
thing stand out. They make the less important things calmer. So the viewer
gets the message in the right order.

Think of a sale poster. You see the big word "SALE" first. Then the discount.
Then the shop name. Then the small details at the bottom. That order is
hierarchy at work.

!!! example "Hierarchy in a simple poster"
    Imagine a poster for a free coding class. The eye should travel like this:

    1. The big title: "Free Coding Class"
    2. The next line: "For students in Sahiwal"
    3. A small line: the address and time

    The title is largest. The address is smallest. Your eye knows what to read
    first without thinking.

## The four tools of hierarchy

You build hierarchy with four simple tools. You can use one, or mix them.

### 1. Size

Bigger things feel more important. The eye goes to the biggest item first.
Make your main message the largest. Make small details actually small.

### 2. Weight

**Weight** (Roman Urdu: harf kitne mote ya patle hain) means how thick or thin
text is. Bold text feels heavier and more important. Thin text feels lighter.
Use bold for the things that matter most.

### 3. Color

A bright or strong color pulls the eye. A soft, dull color stays quiet. Put
your strong color on the one thing you want noticed. If everything is bright,
nothing stands out.

### 4. Space

**Space** (Roman Urdu: cheezon ke ird gird khali jagah) gives an item room to
breathe. When you put empty space around something, it feels important and easy
to see. Crowded things feel less important.

!!! warning "Do not make everything loud"
    New designers often make many things big, bold, and bright at once. Then
    the eye gets confused. Pick one star. Let the rest support it.

!!! tip "tl;dr"
    Design is communication, not decoration. Hierarchy is the order in which
    the eye sees things. You control that order with four tools: size, weight,
    color, and space. Make your main message win the eye first.

## Knowledge check

1. In your own words, what is design for?
2. What is hierarchy?
3. Name the four tools that build hierarchy.
4. Why is "make everything big and bold" a bad idea?

## Optional assignment

Open your phone photo gallery. Take screenshots of three ads you see anywhere,
on apps, on websites, or photos of street banners. For each one, write two
short lines:

1. What does my eye see first?
2. Why does it grab my eye first, size, weight, color, or space?

Save your notes. You are now reading hierarchy like a designer.
